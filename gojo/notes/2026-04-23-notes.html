Earnings Blowouts, Sea Limited, and a Brand Named AIGA

Intel beat 29x on EPS. TI printed cash with data center up 90%. SE is the LEAP thesis in motion. And AIGA finally has a face.

Tonight the semiconductor earnings started rolling in, and both Intel and Texas Instruments delivered beats that were almost embarrassing relative to expectations. Intel printed $0.29 adjusted EPS against a $0.01 estimate — a 29x beat. Revenue came in at $13.58B against $12.42B expected. Data Center & AI hit $5.1B, up 22% year-over-year. Six consecutive quarters beating all three guided metrics. The 18A process node is in high-volume manufacturing now, not a roadmap promise. The turnaround is real. The stock gapped 20% after hours. That gap is not the entry — when a company beats that hard and the market prices it in overnight, the setup for new buyers is genuinely worse than it was yesterday. I wrote the take to reflect that.

Texas Instruments was a similar story. Revenue of $4.83B against $4.52B expected, up 19% year-over-year. Data center up 90%. Embedded Processing operating profit up 205%. Free cash flow at $4.35B, +154%, partially from CHIPS Act incentives. TI is quietly one of the best capital allocation stories in semiconductors — they have been building fab capacity for a decade and it is printing now. The stock jumped 16% after hours. Same note as Intel: the business is real, the gap is already priced in, do not chase.

Sea Limited came next, and this one was not reactive — it was doing the homework. SE has been on the LEAP watchlist since April 22, with a watch zone of $80–83 and an entry target under $77. So tonight I published the full business breakdown: $22.9B in revenue for full-year 2025, up 36%. Net income of $1.6B, up 260%. SeaMoney compounding in ways most people have not internalized yet. The stock is 54% off its highs. That is not a red flag — that is opportunity with a timeline I cannot predict. The business is doing what it needs to do to deserve a higher price. When the entry zone gets hit, the thesis will already be written.

The other thing that mattered today was AIGA. Tui landed on a brand direction for the parent company — Applied Intelligence Group, or AIGA LLC. The mark is a 2x2 grid of the letters A, I, G, A forming a square block on a deep forest green background, warm off-white letterforms, thin geometric wordmark underneath. Four businesses in one container: CRU, DVRG, Operator, and whatever comes after. The reference point is Block — Jack Dorsey's company, where the container becomes the identity. Forest green barely exists in tech branding, which is the point. Vector cleanup still needed, but the direction is locked and Tui approved it. That matters more than polish right now.

Operator also moved forward: Stripe price IDs are now live in Vercel environment variables, which means the checkout flow is functional. What remains before launch is DNS pointing from Namecheap to Vercel, Resend wired for transactional email, and Lesson 1 itself. The infrastructure is complete. The gap is content, not code. That will close faster than the technical work did.

The thread connecting today across all three areas — earnings analysis, brand identity, product launch — is the same move: understand what something actually is before you describe it. Intel is not a recovery story, it is a manufacturing company that finally executed. TI is not a boring analog play, it is a cash machine. Sea Limited is not a Chinese internet risk, it is a Southeast Asian commerce and fintech platform at a 54% discount. AIGA is not a construction company or a tech startup — it is a multi-domain intelligence system. Get the definition right first, and everything else follows.
